Different Sides of the Ever Queen Part 2

A good admonisher, tolerant and open-minded.

Admonition was one of the compulsory lessons for emperors, especially for those who wanted to become a wise ruler—they had to be broad-minded and magnanimous. WU Zetian was one of the emperors who was praised for her “tolerance of others”.

The talented LUO Binwang, who was a master of poetry, drafted an impassioned “Voice for Xu Jingye to denounce WU Zhao” (Wu Zhao=Wu Zetian). When WU Zetian read the lines in it that insulted her for stealing the power soon after her husband’s death, she asked in dismay, “Who wrote this?” Upon learning that it was Luo Binwang, she exclaimed, “How could such a talented man not be the prime minister?” Although Luo Binwang regarded her as an enemy and counted her sins in his essay, WU Zetian was not annoyed, but was still impressed by his talent and mourned the loss of talent.

In response to the phenomenon of redundancy of officials, the scholar SHEN Quanjiao wrote a satirical poem, mocking her for using so many useless people, and was caught by the imperial officer Ji Xianzhi. WU Zetian was not angry, but smiled and said: “Even if you are not redundant officials, what is the use of listening to the opinions of the people? Give him a break!” Such magnanimity made the courtiers feel ashamed of themselves.

WU Zetian also created a special “bronze box”, which had mouths on all sides, and could only put things in, but not take them out. The East Mouth was used for self-nominations and gifts; the South Mouth was used for accepting comments about the government; the West Mouth was used for redressing grievances; and the North Mouth allowed those who had secret plans to speak out about natural phenomena and disasters. The minister of integrity SU Anheng criticized her twice through the bronze box that she was greedy for power and should be ashamed to face the LI family. The two submissions shocked the court, but although WU Zetian did not accept them, she tolerated him and did not punish him.

WU Zetian vigorously advocated Buddhism and spent money on the construction of Buddha statues and Buddhist temples. In the eighth month of the first year of Jiu Shi (700), WU Zetian wanted to build a big statue of Buddha, but was admonished by DI Renjie. In the fourth year of Chang’an (704), Zhang Tinggui, the supervisor of the imperial court, admonished WU Zetian again for making a statue according to Buddhist principles and personnel: “I think that, in the present situation, we should first stabilize the frontier, accumulate provisions and soldiers, and put government affairs first.” After hearing this, WU Zetian stopped building the Buddha and highly praised Zhang Tinggui.

In her old age, WU Zetian wanted to make her nephew, WU Sanshi, the crown prince. Di Renjie said, “If you want to make a crown prince, then you must make a king of Luling.” It so happened that WU Zetian dreamed of a broken parrot’s wing, and Di interpreted the dream for her, using the wing to refer to WU’s two sons. And admonished, “Which is more closely connected, aunt and nephew or mother and son? You make your son a crown prince, and after your death you will be honored. I have never heard of a nephew taking care of his aunt’s funeral.” Thereafter, WU Zetian gradually came to her senses and welcomed Li Xian, King of Luling, back to the palace to be re-installed as crown prince. WU Zetian did not act arbitrarily in the important matter of making a crown prince, and accepted the advice of Di Renjie, making the people happy and the hundred officials support her.

Focusing on agriculture and reducing taxes

WU Zetian had always attached great importance to agriculture, believing that the foundation of a nation lay in agriculture. During her reign, she issued a series of decrees to develop agricultural production, making people stable and prosperous and the economy prosperous and developed.

In the first year of the reign of Emperor Gaozong of the Tang Dynasty (674), WU Zetian made twelve suggestions for ruling the country. The first one was to persuade people to cultivate the land and reduce taxes, showing the importance she attached to agricultural production.

In addition, WU Zetian combined agricultural production with the promotion of officials, rewards and punishments, stipulating that officials could only be promoted if all the fields under their jurisdiction were under cultivation and every household had surplus grain. If the ruling was not good and the number of households was reduced, the officials would face demotion in the year-end evaluation. This urged the local officials to care about people’s livelihood and value agricultural production. At the same time, in order to reduce the burden of the people, WU Zetian ordered several times to reduce taxes and compulsory labour.

During the period when WU Zetian was serving the Emperor Gaozong, she personally organized the writing of the book Zhao Ren Ben Ye Notes, and in the second year of Chui Gong (686), the book was distributed to various places to guide agricultural production. As a textbook of agriculture, it was highly praised by all the Tang emperors.

The development of agriculture was inseparable from the labor force, and WU Zetian took various measures to increase the labor force and increase the enthusiasm for agricultural production. On the one hand, the number of nobles employed was regulated, and the rest were exempted from returning home. On the other hand, for those who ran away from their homes due to famine or migration, they were granted exemptions from servitude to help them return home, and were also allowed to attach their nationality to the local area. In addition, large-scale migration was also carried out to alleviate the problem of scarcity of land and food in some areas. As a result of WU Zetian’s long-standing policy of resting with the people and rewarding agriculture, the country had an abundance of grain and adequate storage.

In addition to the innovation of the imperial examinations, tolerance of admonition, and emphasis on agriculture, WU Zetian’s achievements also include the balance of international and domestic relations. WU Zetian strengthened the external frontier defense, improved the relationship with the various tribes in the northwest, and maintained the smooth flow of the Silk Road between the east and west; internally, she sent troops to suppress rebellions, consolidated national unity, and laid a solid foundation for the “Region of Kai Yuan”, the peak of the Tang Dynasty.
