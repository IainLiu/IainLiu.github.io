Also the Positive Part~~

A Person who Cherish Talents

Some Confucian scholars in the Ming and Qing Dynasties criticized Zhang Juzheng for being narrow-mined and jealous, but that was not the case. In fact, Zhang Juzheng is willing to encourage and support talents, and his rescue of Qu Jiusi, a well-known talent at the time, is the representative event. When Qu was young, he studied with celebrities such as Luo Hongxian and Geng Xiangxiang, and could write profoundly thoughtful articles at the age of fifteen. In the first year of Wanli (1573), Qu and Zhang Jingxiu, the eldest son of the Zhang family, won the provincial graduate of Hu guang area. Friends and villagers congratulated him, and asserted that he will make achievements in learning definitely. When he was still immersed in the joy of obtaining fame, a disaster fell. The local county magistrate Zhang Weihan’s exorbitant deduction of miscellaneous taxes aroused dissatisfaction among the people so people gathered to beat him for their anger. The Zhang Weihan made up a trick and accused Qu Jiusi to confuse people and cause disorder. When the “Qu Jiu Si Injustice Case” happened and Qu was imprisoned, the celebrities spontaneously helped Qu. Tu Long made a “Litigation for Qu” and Feng Mengzhen also defended for Qu to people involved. And the case of Qu Jiusi gradually acknowledged by the court. Once Zhang Juzheng had heard of Qu’s character and behavior from his son, he cherished Qu’s talent very much. He consulted with the two senior officials of Huguang area, Feng Shuji and Wang Zhiyuan, about the case, and replied after understanding the circumstances of the case: “Qu is a disarmed scholar with outstanding talents, but unfortunately framed by an adulterer, which is really pitiful. You law enforcement must deal with it impartially, so that there is no wrongdoing in the world.”

Under the instruction of prime minister, Qu Jiusi was finally acquitted. Huang’an County Chronicles praised this legendary story by saying that “Prime minister takes pity on talents and sweeps away the clouds for him.” Later, Qu wrote Wanli Martial Arts Record, Confucian Temple Ceremony and Music Record, etc, and became a famous scholar of his time, keeping Juzheng’s care and kindness in his heart. Zhang Juzheng, on different occasions and in the eyes of different people in the world, had presented very different characteristics. In the vicissitudes of the situation, he was determined and deep-minded, conspiring to make good judgments, but he was also arbitrary and authoritative. He liked reading, and was suave, unrestrained, cold-blooded and cruel, but sometimes unscrupulous. Combined with greatness and insignificance, ruthlessness and heavy righteousness, Zhang Juzheng is such a long-debated person in history.

His Intelligence

Zhang Juzheng seems always to be the symbol of wisdom. He did not talk too much, but he can always pointed out the key issues with short and accurate word beyond doubt. It was in line with the ancient Chinese saying, “One rarely talk, but his words must be accurate.” Qian Dai, a student of Zhang Juzheng, recalled his teacher in his later years: Zhang was silent all day long, but always hit the point when he spoke. One day, the officials of the left-behind department of Fengyang Capital, where the first emperor of the Ming Dynasty born, traveled long to Beijing and reported important military affairs to the emperor. Generally speaking, officials outside Beijing should first be led by the general political envoy to worship the emperor, and then bow down outside before going on official business. But those left-behind officials directly visited the emperor without waiting for introduction, and was accused of violating the law left by the former emperors. Zhang Juzheng hurriedly stopped accus, and solemnly told them that the lest-behind officials don’t need ushering. The censors disagreed, hoping to come up with legal provisions to refute Zhang Juzheng. But they were dumbfounded after reading the rules of the court, as the prime minister said: no need to ushering for stay-behinders. Once more, the wood at the gate of the capital caught fire. Those censors grasped this chance immediately and wrote the memorials to emperor that the wood suddenly caught fire indicates an unstable society. Zhang Juzheng read those unsubmitted memorials, and said coldly: “Rotten wood can also be used to make fires.” leaving censors speechless calming a meaningless incident.
