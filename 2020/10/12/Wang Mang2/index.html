The story of an "Aline"--Wang Mang Part 2

2020-10-12
Word count: 942   |   Reading time≈ 5 min

In this part, we will explore why Wang Mang’s reform failed.

Wang Mang’s economic reform ended with losing his life.

The state-owned reform of “Wu jun and Liu guan” deprived all the profits of private industry and commerce without bringing the fullness of the national treasury. Following the example of Emperor Wu of the Han Dynasty, Wang Mang appointed a group of businessmen to operate the policy of “Wu jun and Liu guan”. He appointed Sun Qing, the most famous business man in the capital, as the Manager of the Market as well as appointed Zhang Changshu and Xue Zizhong, the wealthy businessmen with “10 million” assets (owning 10 million assets was considered wealthy in ancient China) in Luoyang, as officials controlling prices. Local merchants were usually appointed as franchise officials supervising and regulating commercial activities. Lacking in effective strict supervision mechanism, these merchants took the opportunity to work with administrative officials, cheating on account books to corrupt money from the national treasury. The result was that the government’s treasury was barren, and people’s lives were also miserable.

Four changes in the currency system in seven years have caused financial chaos and the government’s financial credit has almost gone bankrupt. According to history records, every change caused a big bankruptcy among the people, and the prison was overcrowded.

Land nationalization policy had caused collective resistance from interest groups above the middle class. As early as the Western Han Dynasty, land became the main form of wealth. Wang Mang rashly implemented the Wang Tian system without any basis of public opinion, which naturally offended almost all social classes.

At the implementation level, the laxity and non-cooperation of administrative officials are also important reasons for the stagnation of reforms. Wang Mang was so suspicious and superstitious that often excuses officials by earthquakes or solar eclipses. In his reign, he even changed eight chief minister over 10 years.

Therefore, Wang Mang’s reform had offended the interests of aristocracy and the wealthy. Without the support of the proletarians, the central government had not increased its income, leaving emptied hands. By the 14th year of reform, it has been unable to proceed. In 22 AD, Wang Mang had to issue an edict to abolish all reform policies since he took the throne. However, it made no difference. In October of the following year, when the rebels invaded the city of Chang’an, Wang Mang fled to the Weiyang Palace and got killed.

If we compare Wang Mang’s reform with the previous Emperor Wudi’s, we can draw the following technical differences:

For the relationship between economic reform and political reform, both reforms aimed to strengthen centralization, and the premise of economic centralization is political centralization. When Emperor Wudi initiated the reforms, the “Seven Kingdoms Rebellion” had already been suppressed and the feudal vassal had been successfully reduced. Politically, the central government was the most powerful institution, which can ensure the implementation of reform initiatives. However, Wang Mang replaced Han as a relative from the empress’s family, and the legitimacy of the regime has not been resolved. Therefore, radical economic reforms are bound to encounter strong resistance.

In terms of the relationship between reform and popular support, from the game of the four major interest groups, centralization would erode the interests of local governments and the property class. Therefore, the prerequisite for the success of the reform is to obtain the support of the common people. Generally speaking, there are four reasons for centralizers to win the hearts of the people: resisting foreign aggression, preventing local separatism, fighting corruption, and opposing inequality between the rich and the poor. The slogan of Emperor Wudi of the Han for reform was to defend the empire because the war against the Huns was in line with the popular aspirations of the time and won strong public opinion support for the reform. Without political stability and social consensus, Wang Mang hastily initiated reform, which made him lack in popular support. His numerous reform measures, especially the currency reform, all hurt the common people from the beginning.

When it came to the relationship between centralization and private wealth, any centralized economic reform is not to promote productivity in essence, but enable the central government to obtain more economic rights through the redistribution of wealth. Emperor Wudi’s reform was built on the solid foundation of the 70-year resting and restoration, and the long-term recuperation and rejuvenation made a huge room for the state-owned policy to collect wealth. Moreover, wealth hidden in the homes of private merchants was easy to deprive. By contrast, Wang Mang did not get such social conditions. At the end of the Western Han Dynasty, social economy was nearly devastated, and natural disasters continued. Moreover, the huge wealth was held in the official-merchant family groups, which naturally increased the difficulty of collecting wealth.
In the relationship between reform and bureaucratic ability, Emperor Wudi was a prominent emperor with strong force, cruel and ruthless. With thunderous means, he effectively made full use of competent ministers and cruel officials in the reform, using different officials at different reform stages, favoring them deeply, and discarding them after achieving his goal, without procrastination. What’s more, his cooperative relationship with Sang Hongyang lasted more than 40 years, like a pair of political partners. In contrast, Wang Mang was suspicious when choosing his ministers, so he could not built a executive team with one hundred percent loyalty. A major social change was like restructuring a magnificent palace. Wang Mang’s reform lacked systematic consideration risk warning, so its failure was determined.
