Silver--the Lifesaver and Fatal Poison for the Ming Dynasty Part 1

There were always magical links between history events, right? This is astory between the Ming Dynsty and silver. Next I will show you how the silver determined the fate of the Ming Empire. Story in this part is mainly about where did the silver come from…..

In the spring of 1550, Wan Zhu, governor of Zhejiang Province, committed suicide by taking fatally poison.

Three years ago, this highly talented officer was appointed by the Emperor Jiajing, the 11th Emperor of the Ming Dynasty, as the highest military commander in Zhejiang and Fujian provinces, responsible for quelling the Japanese invaders along the southeastern coast.

As soon as he took office, Wan Zhu, who was an upright and honest official, received implies from various interest groups: these groups promised him wealth and higher position in government, as long as he act negatively on control smuggling. However, Wan ignored all these implies, and put much effort in controlling smuggling: “Ban all ferries and private markets.”

At that time, the entire Empire knew that the so-called “Japanese invaders” were mostly people from Zhejiang and Fujian provinces who were both businessmen and criminals and relied on foreign trade to make living. Only a small number of Japanese ronin involved, and these Japanese are often employed by the influential Chinese foreign traders.

Wan Zhu strictly implemented the Imperial Maritime Probation Policy and took effective military measures to capture Dong Xu and Guangtou Li, the leaders of the two largest illegal sea trade groups at that time.

In his report to the Court, Wan Zhu accused prominent families of Zhejiang and Fujian provinces of colluding with Japanese invaders. This report really shocked and fatally threatened prominent families in Zhejiang and Fujian provinces.

The powerful prominent families of the two provinces immediately launched a counterattack against Wan through the Court officials they controlled or sponsored. The Court officials of Zhejiang and Fujian tried to impeach Wan. On the one hand, they said that both Xu dong and Li Guangtou who had been captured were all good citizens, not smugglers, and had no contacts with Japanese invaders. On the other hand, the accused Wan of slandered the notable citizens of Zhejiang and Fujian provinces, confusing the Emperor’s judgement (a heave crime in ancient China). They also asked the Emperor to take back his military power.

Well, as a result, Wan ordered the death penalty for 96 captured “Japanese invaders” and “pirates.”

This action heavily frightened officials in Zhejiang and Fujian provinces. They impeached Wan for “Unrestricted and unauthorized killing”——in the imperial era, only the Emperor can determine people’s death. Wan killed those 96 people unauthorizedly, giving his rivals evidences to impeach him for misreporting military achievements and arrogated Emperor’s power.

Soon after such impeachments, Wan, the talented official and the hero of maritime probation, became a criminal.

Staying in the prison, with complex emotions, he said: “It is easy for me to to eliminate foreign scoundrels, but hard for me to kick the vile Chinese off; it is far easy for me to eliminate foreign invaders and pirates, but it is even far more hard for me to beat scumbags in the Court.”

Desperately, Wan Zhu committed suicide after taking poison. Before that, he felt helpless: “Though the Emperor wanted me alive, officials from Zhejiang and Fujian provinces would managed to make me a dead body.”

I read this story on a paper discussing why Wan Zhu’s maritime probation policy failed ultimately. This is a tragedy. A really talented and upright official died for a out-of-date policy.

But dude, I’m not gonna talk about matters related to maritime probation. Conversely, I wanna show you something after Ming Dynasty’s opening-up, so the tragedy above can be considered as a context. I can show you my topic here: Silver determined the fate of an Empire.

Seventeen years after Wan Zhu’s death, in 1567, the Emperor Longqing, the 12th Emperor of the Ming Dynasty, acceded throne. He went with the tide and initiated the “Longqing opening-up,” abolished the maritime probation. This led to a series of chain reaction, and the most significant point is that the silver can ultimately legally unrestrainedly flowing into China.

From this time, the Ming dynasty entered its “Silver Age.”

But, like I said before, the empire’s destiny was also started being determined by silver.

In 1542, three merchant ships were captured on route that connect Japan and Zhangzhou, China, with 3,000 kilograms of silver on board. At that time, there were about 10 to 20 such merchant ships engaged in Sino-Japanese trade at sea, importing more than 20,000 kilograms of silver into China. But these are all illegal smuggling.

During the region of Emperor Jiajing, the serious Japanese invasion was triggered in large by the discovery of huge silver deposits in Japan. Merchant ships from the coasts of Zhejiang, Fujian and Guangdong provinces sailed successively to Kyushu of Japan to sell products and received huge amount of silver.

According to historians’ estimates, from 1540 to 1644, an average of 75 tons of silver flowed into China from Japan each year, totaling about 7,500 tons.

Well, here I need to make one thing clear: That was merely one way for silver flowing into China.
Another source of silver was America.

In 1545 and 1548, Spanish colonists successively discovered huge silver mines in Potosi (Now a part of Bolivia) and Zacatecas in Mexico. Since then, large quantities of low-cost American silver have been continuously transported into Spain and other major European countries through international trade, financial manipulation, smuggling, pirate plunder and war reparations.

But most of the American silver that went into Europe was eventually shipped to China to buy Chinese products.

The Portuguese merchant ship alone carries 6000 to 30000 kilograms of silver annually from their port to Macao.

Statistics showed that between a third and a half of the world’s silver production, including Japan and the Americas, ended up in China in the late Ming dynasty. The Ming Dynasty was thus described as the world’s “Silver suction pump,” absorbing huge amounts of the world’s silver at that time.

Well, the fact is that the competitiveness of Chinese manufacturing was matchless at that time. The whole world benefited from China’s export of high-quality and inexpensive commodities, tea, porcelain, silk, cotton, medicinal herbs and so on. But for China, there are very few goods that can be sold to China, which forces foreign countries to trade with China only use silver as currency.

No wonder the Emperors of the Qing Dynasty habitually declared that “China is in a prominent country that process various things, and we don’t need to trade with you.” After all, China ran a tremendous trade surplus for three or four hundred years until 1800.

Let me give you an interesting example. In 1637, in the tenth year of Emperor Chongzhen, the last Emperor of the Ming Dynasty, the merchant ship of the British East India Company arrived in China for the first time. The British people were so excited that they thought they could make a lot of money from the Chinese people. In the end they could not sell even a single piece of English goods, but spent 80000 Spanish silver dollars and returned loaded with Chinese goods.

Now we know that during the Ming Dynasty, huge amount of silver were flowing into China. So the next weeks I gonna show you how the silver changed the empire’s destiny.
