Bank note in the Yuan Dynasty and Ming Dynasty

2019-10-16
Word count: 1.2k   |   Reading time≈ 7 min

Though we said Jiaozi was virtually a supplement of copper standard and a tool for grabbing citizens’ wealth, dynasties later were continuing issuing bank notes.

The Yuan Dynasty followed the Song Dynasty and continued to issue banknotes, but the situation was not much better. In terms of the Yuan monetary system, the “Jiao” notes and “Bao” notes were for the successive issuance of two kinds of paper money, which were the paper money issued under two different proposals, perhaps it should be said that there were two different value symbols, “Jiao” notes was the value of silk symbols, “Bao” notes was the value of money symbols, but both were also on the silver price, and silver to cash. It can be seen, this is a kind of “neither fish nor fowl” paper money system with copper standard, Pali standard, silver standard, paper standard traces of all. In any case, it could not be understood as a silver standard which was difficult to define the boundaries of the standard. But a glimpse from the Yuan history records can be seen although the Yuan monetary system is indeterminate, but this era was dominated by the circulation of paper money, with silver and copper circulation not allowed undoubtedly. In short, the Yuan monetary system was still a monetary monstrosity inherited from the Song Dynasty—for instance, the “Zhongtong” banknotes and “Zhiyuan” banknotes are only priced against silver; “Zhida” banknotes are priced against silver and copper at the same time; “Zhizheng” banknotes are only priced against copper. Isn’t this agricultural monetary culture (copper currency culture) on the compatibility of pastoral monetary culture? Therefore, paper money in the Yuan dynasty was still the same as the Song dynasty as a deformed form of money, with paper money for the table, copper standard for the inside, which is the perspective of the confusing Yuan monetary system and the conclusion.

In ancient China, the characteristics of the Chinese economy and monetary culture made it impossible to implement a complete copper standard (iron standard), a silver standard or even a paper standard because of the paper money standard’s delayed and precocious nature. The pure copper standard was proved flaw by the shortage of copper; the iron standard was limited by the inconvenience of circulation; the silver standard was restricted by the level of economic development and silver production; and the paper money system was repeatedly driven by the financial will that pushed it to collapse, which means only a mixed monetary system could work out. At the end of the Yuan Dynasty, because there was no solid support (sufficient copper or iron), the indiscriminate issuance of paper money led to serious inflation and bankruptcy of countless peasants, which directly led to the end of the Yuan Dynasty and the establishment of the Ming Dynasty.

At the beginning of the Ming Dynasty, Zhu Yuanzhang continued the policy of the Yuan Dynasty by implementing a monstrous paper currency policy. Again, without the support of the solid sufficient base, just fifty years later, the Ming paper currency was devalued more than 10,000 times. At the same time, until the middle of the Ming Dynasty, the government strictly restricted the trade and circulation of private silver. Here we have to mention two important reforms in the history of the Ming Dynasty.

The first was the “Long Qing Opening-up” during the reign of Emperor Long Qing (1566-1572). The Long Qing Opening-up allowed foreign trade and turned China into a “silver pump”—China had absorbed nearly two-thirds of the world’s silver reserves in just a decade. The second point is the great reformer Zhang Juzheng’s “a-whip law”, which directly stipulated that henceforth local taxes paid to the central government were settled in silver. At this point, the status of silver in China’s currency took a big formal leap. However, if we examine the Ming monetary system closely, we will find that it was not a formal “silver standard” as some posts and writers claim. Although the Ming Dynasty’s monetary system has reflected the importance of silver, in essence it was still copper standard, and continued the deformed pattern of mutual coupling of banknotes and copper standard. The early Ming the phenomenon that “Bao” notes devalued but the weight of copper increased had already taken place. Faced with the same problem as the Yuan Dynasty, the Ming dynasty solved it the same way: by banning the circulation of copper coins to keep treasure money in use. In the sixteenth year of Chenghua (1481), Qiu Jun’s (Qiu Jun: Head of the Minister of Treasure and member of the Cabinet) currency reform plan was apparently a silver standard, but in essence it was a mix of copper and paper money standard. Ming dynasty had implemented the silver standard, which was to plunder silver from citizens and replenish monetary quantities. When Li Zicheng, leader of the peasant rebelling destroyed the Ming Dynasty, entered Beijing, it was said that the late Ming Dynasty no individual would like to exchange silver for paper money. Once people said: “Although the people were stupid, who were willing to buy a paper with a gold.” The use of silver in society was to avoid the devaluation of paper money and copper shortage. After the middle of the Ming Dynasty, silver’s status as currency was enhanced. After the reign of Emperor Jiajing (1522-1566), the usage of silver grew, and gradually formed the system that silver was used in large-quantity trade while copper was used in small-quantity trade. Under such system, both silver and copper standard were adopted in parallel, which started the precedent of the Qing dynasty monetary system. However, the silver standard is still not independent from the copper standard.

The increasing use of silver after the middle Ming Dynasty was not an intrinsic requirement of economic development, but a product of the inability of the copper and banknote mixed currency pattern to develop normally and support the operation of economic life. Objectively speaking, it is difficult to reconcile the use of silver coins and the pattern of folk use of copper with the level of economic development. Subjectively speaking, the increasing use of silver contradicted the “copper fetishism” mentioned above. The Ming History set out that, in early years of Long Qing, there were people claiming that it was necessary to make people rich, which needed to value cloth, silk, beans and corn and belittle silver. That to belittle silver must use copper money is a reflection of this pattern. It was at this time that the West’s monetary power has extended to the territory of China. In 1518 A.D., the Spanish king minted a double-pillar silver dollar. With the influx of Spanish trade into China, the Spanish silver coin was the prelude to a head-on conflict between Chinese and Western monetary culture. In the face of the challenge of Western monetary culture, the historical fate of the Chinese monetary system is easy to imagine.
